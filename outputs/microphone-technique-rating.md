# Microphone Technique Rating

This is a 21-minute voice note, so the analysis and scoring below are based on the overall performance in this file.

### Scoring
1. **Mic distance consistency (5/10):** The speaker moves back and forth from the mic frequently. You can hear the tone shift from proximity to echoey, indicating drifting.
2. **Plosive management (6/10):** Most were managed, but there were a few noticeable pops on words like "basically" and "problematic."
3. **Sibilance control (6/10):** The 'S' and 'T' sounds are quite sharp at times and could be de-essed.
4. **Handling noise (3/10):** There is significant handling noise. It sounds like the speaker is walking while recording. There are also constant bumps and taps.
5. **Room selection and acoustic environment (3/10):** It sounds like a large, untreated, empty room. There is noticeable reverb throughout the recording.
6. **Mouth noise management (4/10):** There are frequent clicks, smacks, and some labored breathing/swallowing close to the mic.
7. **Breath noise management (5/10):** Some breaths are sharp and audible because the mic is being used in an handheld fashion that doesn't isolate the mouth from the nose/breath path.
8. **Volume consistency (6/10):** There is drift between sections where the speaker's engagement increases and decreases.
9. **Head movement discipline (6/10):** Generally on-axis, but the movement results in noticeable phase shift and volume dips.
10. **Overall recording hygiene (4/10):** The recording lacks the stability and preparation of a controlled studio environment.

### Overall Score: 4.8/10

### Summary
The speaker is articulate and energetic, but the audio quality is hampered by an unsuitable environment (untreated room) and a lack of mic discipline (walking/handling). The constant background environmental cues make the recording sound like an unedited, spontaneous voice note rather than professional content.

### 3 Tips for Immediate Improvement:

1.  **Stop Moving:** Don't walk while recording. Anchor yourself in one place and keep the microphone stationary on a desk stand. The sound of footsteps and the fluctuating distance between your mouth and the mic are creating major audio issues.
2.  **Choose a Dead Room:** Avoid large or tiled spaces. Move into a smaller room filled with soft items like curtains, cushions, or a carpet. Even recording inside a walk-in closet full of clothes will dramatically reduce the "echoey" reverb and make the voice sound much more professional.
3.  **Use a Pop Filter or "The Pencil Hack":** If you can't buy a dedicated pop filter, angle the mic slightly away from your direct breath path (so you are speaking across the capsule rather than directly into it). Also, be aware of "s" sounds; keeping the mic slightly off-center helps mitigate that harsh sibilance without sacrificing the full, rich tone of your voice.
